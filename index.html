WHO WE ARE

Food Cooked With Memory

Baladi began at a kitchen table — a single home cook stirring a pot of mulukhiyah the way her mother taught her, the way her grandmother taught hers. Every recipe we serve carries a lineage, a small inheritance of patience, smoke, and spice.

We cook the dishes that fill Egyptian homes during weddings, holidays, and ordinary Friday afternoons. The slow-simmered tagines. The hand-rolled grape leaves. The kunafa pulled apart at the table. Nothing is rushed. The blends are toasted whole and ground the same morning we use them.

What started as cooking for neighbors has grown into cooking for tables across the city — but the standard hasn't changed. If we wouldn't serve it to family, we don't send it out.

"Every dish carries a name, a memory, and a table it was made for."
